\section{Overview}
This document outlines the governing equations and numerical algorithms implemented in the \textbf{Gasflash} backend. The solver has been rigorously cross-referenced with the \textit{Space Propulsion Notes} (Academic Reference) to ensure physical accuracy for high-speed compressible flows, including nozzle expansion, friction losses (Fanno), and thermal forcing (Rayleigh).

\section{Governing Equations (1D Euler with Source Terms)}
The backend solves the quasi-1D unsteady Euler equations using a finite-volume approach (Roe-MUSCL scheme):
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \mathbf{U}}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial x} = \mathbf{S}
\end{equation}
where the state vector $\mathbf{U}$, flux $\mathbf{F}$, and source term $\mathbf{S}$ are:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{U} = \begin{bmatrix} \rho A \\ \rho u A \\ \rho E A \end{bmatrix}, \quad 
    \mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix} \rho u A \\ (\rho u^2 + p) A \\ (\rho E + p) u A \end{bmatrix}, \quad
    \mathbf{S} = \begin{bmatrix} \dot{m}_{source} \\ p \frac{dA}{dx} - \tau_w \pi D \\ \dot{m}_{source} h_{total, source} + \dot{Q}_{heat} \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\section{Influence Coefficient Matrix}
The solver's behavior for steady-state perturbations follows the generalized influence matrix derived in the notes. For a differential change in Mach number $M^2$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dM^2}{M^2} = F_A \frac{dA}{A} + F_Q \frac{dT_0}{T_0} + F_f \frac{4f dx}{D} + F_w \frac{d\dot{m}}{\dot{m}}
\end{equation}
Verified coefficients implemented in \texttt{influence\_solver.py}:
\begin{align*}
    F_A &= -\frac{2(1 + \frac{\gamma-1}{2}M^2)}{1-M^2} \\
    F_Q &= \frac{1+\gamma M^2}{1-M^2} \\
    F_f &= \frac{\gamma M^2(1+\frac{\gamma-1}{2}M^2)}{1-M^2} \\
    F_w &= \frac{2(1+\gamma M^2)(1+\frac{\gamma-1}{2}M^2)}{1-M^2}
\end{align*}

\section{Physical Regimes \& Solvers}
\subsection{Isentropic Nozzle Flow}
The area-Mach relationship is used to detect choking and initialize the CFD field:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{A}{A^*} = \frac{1}{M} \left[ \frac{2}{\gamma+1} \left( 1 + \frac{\gamma-1}{2}M^2 \right) \right]^{\frac{\gamma+1}{2(\gamma-1)}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Fanno Flow (Friction)}
Friction is modeled via the Fanning factor $f$. The momentum sink $\tau_w \pi D$ is integrated implicitly for numerical stability. The solver correctly captures the $M \to 1$ trend in both subsonic and supersonic regimes.

\subsection{Rayleigh Flow (Heat Addition)}
Heat addition $\dot{Q}$ drives the flow towards Mach 1. The solver supports both fixed total enthalpy jumps ($\Delta h_0$) and localized heat flux ($q$). The temperature peak at $M = 1/\sqrt{\gamma}$ is accurately resolved.

\subsection{Mass Addition (Solid Rocket Motor)}
Vieille's Law is implemented for solid grain components:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{m} = \rho_s A_b a (P/10^6)^n
\end{equation}
Note: The pressure $P$ is converted to MPa to match standard propellant burn-rate coefficients.

\section{Shock Localization (Shooting Method)}
For overexpanded nozzles or ducts with high backpressure, the \texttt{iterative\_solver.py} employs a shooting method to find the shock location $x_s$. It optimizes:
\begin{equation}
    f(x_s) = P_{exit}(x_s) - P_{ambient} = 0
\end{equation}
using a bracketed Brent solver. This ensures that the normal shock is placed exactly where the pressure recovery matches the boundary condition.

\section{Real Gas Model (Van der Waals)}
The solver implements a Van der Waals EoS for high-density gases:
\begin{equation}
    P = \frac{\rho R T}{1 - \rho b} - a \rho^2, \quad a^2 = \frac{\gamma_{eff} R T}{(1-\rho b)^2} - 2 a \rho
\end{equation}
This allows simulation of real-gas effects in high-pressure combustion chambers.
